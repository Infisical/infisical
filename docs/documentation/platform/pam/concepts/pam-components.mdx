Let's say you want to give your platform team secure access to your production databases. Here's how you'd set that up in Infisical PAM.

## Start with Accounts

An **account** represents a single database or server you want to manage. You provide the connection details (host, port, database) and credentials (username, password), and Infisical stores them securely.

For example, you might create an account called `orders-db` for your orders database, and another called `users-db` for your users database.

But you probably don't want these accounts floating around individually — you want to organize them.

## Organize with Folders

**Folders** group related accounts together. You might create a folder called `prod-databases` and put both `orders-db` and `users-db` inside it.

Folders serve two purposes:
1. **Organization** — keep related accounts together
2. **Access control** — when you grant someone access to a folder, they get access to everything inside it

So if you grant your platform team access to the `prod-databases` folder, they can connect to both databases. You don't have to grant access to each account individually.

Now, you might want different rules for how people connect to these databases — maybe production databases should require MFA, while development databases don't. That's where templates come in.

## Define Rules with Templates

**Templates** define the rules that apply when someone connects to an account:
- How long can they stay connected?
- Do they need to provide a reason?
- Do they need to complete MFA?
- Should the session be recorded?

Every account uses a template. Infisical provides default templates (`postgres`, `ssh`) with sensible settings, so you can start creating accounts immediately.

When you need different rules, create a custom template. For example, you might create a `prod-postgres` template that requires MFA and limits sessions to 4 hours, while keeping the default `postgres` template for development databases.

The key insight: **templates define rules, folders define access**. You can have accounts in the same folder using different templates (different rules, same team access), or accounts in different folders using the same template (same rules, different team access).

## Control Access with Memberships

**Memberships** determine who can access what. You assign a user or group a **role** on a folder or account.

The roles are:
- **Admin** — full control (create accounts, manage access, connect)
- **Connector** — can connect to accounts
- **Requester** — can request access to accounts
- **Auditor** — can view sessions and logs (read-only)

When you assign a membership on a folder, it applies to all accounts inside. For most cases, this is all you need — grant your team access to a folder and they can reach everything in it.

For exceptions (like giving a contractor access to just one database), you can assign a membership directly on an individual account.

## Putting It All Together

Back to our example: giving your platform team secure access to production databases.

1. **Create a template** called `prod-postgres` with your production rules (4-hour sessions, MFA required, recording enabled) — or use the default if it fits your needs
2. **Create a folder** called `prod-databases`
3. **Add accounts** to the folder (`orders-db`, `users-db`), each using the `prod-postgres` template
4. **Grant your team access** by assigning them the Connector role on the `prod-databases` folder

Now your team can connect to any database in that folder, and every session follows your production rules.
